International Workshop on
Fairness in Algorithmic Decision-Making for Education

to be held as part of the 27th International Conference on Artificial Intelligence in Education (AIED 2026) and the Festival of Learning 2026

Workshops: 27–28 June 2026 · Coex, Seoul, Republic of Korea (with support for remote attendance where offered)

Workshop Aims and Scope

Applications of artificial intelligence in education are increasingly scrutinized for the fairness of their automated decision-making (e.g., auto-grading, problem sequencing, personalization, interventions). Yet comparatively little attention has been paid to the fairness of the procedures by which those decisions are made—i.e., procedural fairness. That matters: even outcomes that look fair on paper can be objectionable to students, teachers, and other stakeholders if the process is unfair. Without a structured approach, AIED systems risk perpetuating bias, deepening inequality, and sidelining minority voices.

This half-day Fair4AIED workshop takes further steps toward an operational blueprint that systematically integrates fairness—including procedural fairness and algorithmic audits—into AIED research, practice, and adoption. The program engages participants through lightning talks, Q&A, abstract-based discussions, grounding rounds in small groups, and a world café oriented toward drafting and synthesizing the blueprint.

We build on a community-driven view of fairness: defining what fairness means in context, identifying at-risk groups, turning fairness into measurable constructs, mitigating bias, reporting fairness analyses in research, and auditing AIED systems—extending to generative AI and other emerging decision systems, alongside research and regulatory perspectives (e.g., the EU Artificial Intelligence Act as one example of governance context).

We welcome researchers, practitioners, policymakers, and industry / edtech professionals—roles that together shape how fairness is designed, governed, and experienced in real educational settings.

Workshop Topics

We invite abstracts (see Submission for the 250-word format) to seed discussion toward the blueprint. Submissions should relate to fairness in algorithmic decision-making for education. Cross-cutting themes emphasized in the program include: defining fairness in educational contexts; identifying at-risk or marginalized groups; measurement, metrics, and evaluation; bias mitigation; transparency and reporting of fairness analyses; and auditing AIED systems—with attention to both outcome fairness and procedural fairness.

The following areas illustrate the scope (not an exhaustive list):

  • Fairness methods design and incorporation
    • Incorporating fairness principles across an AIED system
    • Ensuring fairness across the AIED system lifecycle
    • Integrating fairness with other AIED areas to provide complementary benefits
  • Fairness auditing and evaluation
    • Algorithmic auditing procedures in the context of AIED
    • Improving fairness metrics, definitions, and evaluation approaches in AIED
    • Strengthening mechanisms for long-term adherence to fairness in AIED
  • Fairness gaps and challenges identification
    • Identifying gaps and challenges in AIED regarding fairness
    • Exploring what we can do better concerning fairness as a research community
    • Challenges to improving fairness in practice
    • Methods for addressing these challenges in adopting fairness
  • Procedural Fairness
    • Operationalizing and studying procedural fairness across an AIED system
    • Gaps in current AIED research related to procedural fairness
    • Ensuring procedural fairness in AIED by identifying and addressing practical implementation challenges
    • Topics related to procedural fairness in education

Submission Details

We invite authors to submit a 250-word abstract, written in English. Abstracts should not exceed the indicated word limit. References do not count toward the word limit.

  • Abstracts should be submitted using the submission form (link to be announced).
  • All submissions will undergo a single-blind review process conducted by the organizers.
  • Submissions will be evaluated based on relevance, originality, significance, and clarity.
  • The accepted abstracts will be made available on the workshop website.
  • At least one author per abstract must register for and attend the workshop to present it.
  • Accepted abstracts will be presented as lightning talks of approximately 5 minutes, each followed by Q&A; after the lightning session, presenters will host table discussions of their work as described under Program.
  • Presenters are responsible for preparing slides for their talk.

We expect authors, the program committee, and the organizing committee to adhere to the ACM’s Conflict of Interest Policy and the ACM’s Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct.

Program

The workshop is a half-day, hybrid-friendly event. Exact times and remote access (e.g., videoconference link) will be posted when available. The structure below follows the accepted workshop design for the Festival of Learning / AIED 2026, compressed into a single half-day session.

Part 1

  • Keynote talk — Advances and emerging challenges in fairness for AIED.
  • Lightning talks — Short slide-based talks from participants, based on accepted abstracts (~5 minutes each, plus dedicated Q&A).
  • Abstracts’ discussion — Presenters host tables; other participants move between tables to discuss each abstract.
  • Interim summary — Brief recap of key ideas from the keynote and lightning session (also shared as a take-home flier to support networking and the rest of the workshop).

Part 2

  • Opening briefing — Objectives, structure, and activities for Part 2; organizers may use Slido (or similar) to learn about participants and form diverse small groups (typically 4–10 people per group, for roughly 4–6 groups depending on attendance).
  • Grounding round(s) — Groups analyze thought-provoking prompts from technical, ethical, and practical angles (e.g., “What should an operational framework for fairness in AI for education include, from an interdisciplinary perspective?”). Groups capture challenges, solutions, and open questions; a moderator facilitates; groups report back (e.g., shared document, slides, or whiteboard). Additional rounds may be held if time allows, with groups rotating prompts.
  • World café — Organizers introduce the methodology (rules, goals, structure) to consolidate insights toward an actionable blueprint. Each table, with a host, focuses on a section of the blueprint; participants rotate between tables across rounds (recap, discussion, and drafting of takeaways and actions).
  • Closing — Table hosts summarize; chairs lead synthesis, refinement of the blueprint, and discussion of follow-up steps.
